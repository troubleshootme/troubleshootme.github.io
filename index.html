  • Dry Van | 24,314 loads | $2.45 paid

    • Tactics:
    • Avoid long-haul one-way van freight into fuel-heavy or weather-stressed regions unless customer approval is already in hand
    • Sell wider appointment windows
    • Source early on any Midwest or Northern Tier freight

  • Reefer | 8,396 loads | $3.06 paid

    • What it means: Reefer is the clearest carrier-pricing market on the board. A +$0.27/mile spread says carriers are winning negotiations after the post.
    • Best use today: Southern produce, PNW nursery-adjacent freight, and any service-sensitive food or pharma moves.
    • Broker edge: Pre-cover before quoting. If you quote first, you will often be behind the market by the time the customer approves.
    • Tactics:
    • Lock reefer fuel responsibility in writing
    • Confirm washout terms before dispatch
    • Treat northern transcon reefer as premium freight, not standard freight

  • Flatbed | 88,378 loads | $3.02 paid

    • What it means: Flatbed remains the center of gravity in this market.
    • Best use today: Construction, infrastructure, steel, machinery, and data-center-related project freight.
    • Broker edge: Qualified carrier access beats rate shopping.
    • Tactics:
    • Lead with securement-capable incumbents
    • Do not quote before confirming tarping, dimensions, site conditions, and loading equipment
    • Push customers to preload and reduce on-site dwell

  • Heavy Haul | 43,043 loads | $3.01 paid

    • What it means: The fact that paid is slightly below posted suggests this is not a blind overpay market; it is a precision market.
    • Broker edge: Clean specs and permit readiness can still create negotiating room.
    • Tactics:
    • Quote only after exact dimensions, axle needs, and permit status are confirmed
    • Use longer lead-time language
    • Protect escort, route-survey, and permit-delay terms

  • Specialized | 21,195 loads | $2.76 paid

    • What it means: Tight, but rational.
    • Broker edge: Exact equipment matching is where margin lives.
    • Tactics:
    • Match step deck, RGN (Removable Gooseneck), conestoga, or stretch equipment precisely
    • Avoid “close enough” trailer assumptions
    • Bundle repeat freight with proven niche carriers

  • LTL/Partial | 11,142 loads | $1.71 paid

    • What it means: This is a useful customer-retention valve for price-sensitive freight, especially when full truckload pricing shocks the customer.
    • Broker edge: Turn resistance into alternatives instead of losing the shipment.
    • Tactics:
    • Offer partials where transit flexibility exists
    • Set expectations on delay risk in weather-affected Midwest lanes
    • Use for retail replenishment and non-urgent manufacturing freight

💰 Margin protection framework for today

  • Separate the premiums.

    • Why it matters: When brokers quote one all-in number, they usually end up eating the adjustment when fuel or weather changes intraday.
    • Use these buckets:
    • Base linehaul
    • Fuel
    • Weather/disruption premium
    • Appointment-flex or service premium

  • Shorten quote validity.

    • Best practice today: On Washington, Montana, Wyoming, Wisconsin, and reefer freight, treat quotes as short-duration approvals, not all-day commitments.

  • Use service tiers with customers.

    • Standard option: Wider pickup/delivery windows, lower premium
    • Premium option: Tight appointment protection, backup planning, and priority carrier sourcing

    This reduces customer resistance because they feel they are choosing service level, not just absorbing a rate increase.

  • Do not chase bad volume.

    • Bad volume today: One-way long-haul freight into high-fuel or weather-stressed markets with rigid appointments and no surcharge flexibility.
    • Good volume today: Reload-friendly open-deck, preapproved reefer, partial conversions, and rescue freight from weaker brokers.
