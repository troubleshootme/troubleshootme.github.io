🧠 The five forces driving today’s brokerage decisions

  • 1) Fuel shock is removing marginal capacity $5.375/gallon diesel raises the minimum acceptable economics for every trip. Carriers are not just asking for more money; they are rejecting bad trip design:

    • low-mile short hauls without reload visibility
    • appointment-heavy freight with unpaid dwell risk
    • mountain/weather exposure without premium
    • long-haul loads where the fuel burden hits immediately

  • 2) Weather is distorting truck positioning The Oregon Cascades storm and Wyoming wind event matter because they disrupt future availability, not only today’s movement.

    • A truck delayed today may miss tomorrow’s reload
    • A carrier that avoids a risky routing may disappear from an entire regional pool for 24-72 hours
    • In western New York, the cost is more likely to show up as dock delay and appointment failure than a hard highway shutdown

  • 3) Open-deck seasonality is now in charge Flatbed at 75,083 loads is the center of gravity. Spring construction, industrial freight, and project cargo are absorbing the most operationally disciplined carriers. That means ordinary brokerage habits do not work:

    • vague tarp instructions
    • incomplete securement assumptions
    • unclear loading hours
    • “we’ll figure out accessorials later”

  • 4) Solvency enforcement is shifting trust economics In tight markets, the carrier chooses the broker. In a fuel shock market, the carrier chooses the broker with:

    • credible payment
    • clean dispatch
    • few disputes
    • fast paperwork handling
    • low surprise factor The FMCSA solvency rule makes those trust signals even more valuable.

  • 5) Shippers will resist broad rate increases but accept external logic Customers rarely respond well to “the market feels tight.” They respond better to:

    • national average rate at $2.65/mile
    • diesel at $5.375/gallon
    • USPS implementing an 8% fuel surcharge
    • weather-driven service risk on specific corridors The psychology matters: external evidence reduces rate-pushback.
