🧭 Savvy Broker's Playbook

🔑 Executive Signal Summary

  • This is a weekend pause, not a true loosening. Total available loads fell to 155,883 from 177,566 (-12.2%), but the national average rate is still $2.66/mile, which remains well above one week ago at $2.58/mile and one month ago at $2.34/mile. That tells me the market is still structurally firm even if the board looks lighter this morning.

  • The real market is being driven by execution risk, not just raw load count. Diesel at $5.67/gallon, Midwest flooding, and a short operating window in western Michigan mean carriers are choosing freight based on margin quality, route certainty, and reload logic.

  • Open-deck still owns the money. Flatbed, heavy haul, and specialized combine for 120,733 loads, or roughly 77.5% of all posted opportunity. If a brokerage is not leaning hard into industrial, project, machinery, steel, building products, and trailer-specific freight today, it is leaving the best margin pools untouched.

  • Board pricing is overstating actual executable rates on most freight. Paid rates are below posted rates in every equipment class today:

    • Van: $2.36 paid vs. $2.46 posted
    • Reefer: $2.83 paid vs. $2.85 posted
    • Flatbed: $3.07 paid vs. $3.17 posted
    • Heavy Haul: $3.14 paid vs. $3.19 posted
    • Specialized: $2.56 paid vs. $2.92 posted
    • LTL/Partial: $1.65 paid vs. $1.76 posted

  • The most important read: this is not a market where you blindly chase every ask. It is a market where ordinary freight can still be bought under posted, while weather-hit, appointment-sensitive, or premium-equipment freight will punish underpricing fast.

📈 What the board is really saying beneath the surface

  • Market firmness is coming from usable capacity, not broad demand.

    • Today: 155,883 loads, $2.66/mile
    • Yesterday: 177,566 loads, $2.70/mile
    • One week ago: 141,006 loads, $2.58/mile
    • One month ago: 178,627 loads, $2.34/mile

  • Key interpretation: compared with last week, the market has more loads and meaningfully higher rates. Compared with yesterday, it has fewer weekend loads and only a slight rate softening. That is typical of a market that is still tight but temporarily quieter.

  • The paid-vs-posted gap matters today.

    • Broad takeaway: load board asking prices are running ahead of what the broader market is actually clearing.
    • Broker implication: on standard freight, disciplined procurement should beat the board.
    • Important exception: on flood-affected Midwest lanes, produce-linked reefer, and hard appointment freight, do not assume the national paid average is your buy target. Those lanes will price above the average because failure cost is higher.

  • Specialized is the cleanest pricing anomaly on the screen.

    • Posted: $2.92/mile
    • Paid: $2.56/mile
    • Gap: -$0.36/mile
    • What that usually means: a lot of “specialized” freight is being posted with fuzzy specs, wrong trailer assumptions, or seller optimism. This is a broker edge day: the desk that verifies trailer type, dimensions, securement, and unload method will buy smarter than competitors.

  • Heavy haul is quietly one of the strongest signals today.

    • Volume only dipped from 36,088 to 35,552 (-1.5%), much less than the overall market.
    • That tells you infrastructure and project freight is stubbornly resilient, even during a weekend slowdown.

🚛 Mode-by-mode broker playbook

🟧 Flatbed

  • Market read: 68,800 loads at $3.07/mile paid
  • What matters: this remains the largest and richest spot segment on the board.
  • Broker edge today:
    • Prioritize Monday freight now.
    • Open-deck gets hit harder than van when wind, wet roads, and detours compound.
    • Favor shippers with fast loading and clear securement requirements.
    • Carriers will accept a slightly lower linehaul if turn time is clean.
    • Quote with detour and weather language.
    • Flood-related route creep and Sunday wind exposure can erase margin fast.
  • Best use of time: call your top 20 flatbed carriers before they get fully committed to Monday project freight.

🟫 Heavy Haul

  • Market read: 35,552 loads at $3.14/mile paid
  • What matters: this is a planning market, not a panic market.
  • Broker edge today:
    • Do not quote until dimensions, weight, axle need, permit path, and escort requirements are confirmed.
    • Route around Midwest flood friction before you tender.
    • Protect appointment windows aggressively.
  • Why it wins: heavy haul carriers tolerate price negotiation better than spec ambiguity.

🟪 Specialized

  • Market read: 16,381 loads at $2.56/mile paid
  • What matters: the largest gap between posted and paid rates says the market is misdescribing freight.
  • Broker edge today:
    • Validate whether the load is truly specialized or simply inconvenient.
    • Confirm trailer type, commodity dimensions, securement, and unload method before quoting.
    • Use this mode for margin discipline, not for volume chasing.
  • Practical rule: if the shipper cannot explain why it needs a specific trailer, the first quote is probably wrong.

🚐 Dry Van

  • Market read: 19,818 loads at $2.36/mile paid
  • What matters: van is selective, not soft.
  • Broker edge today:
    • Buy ordinary freight under the ask where routing is clean.
    • Sell premium on Midwest and appointment-sensitive freight where service failure is expensive.
    • Use regional carriers who are already positioned near freight.
  • Best targets: replenishment freight, short-haul turns, and routing-guide fallout tied to rising OTRI (Outbound Tender Rejection Index) in stressed regions.

🧊 Reefer

  • Market read: 7,088 loads at $2.83/mile paid
  • What matters: reefer is still expensive, and the tiny gap versus posted means the market is being quoted fairly tightly up front.
  • Broker edge today:
    • Book only after confirming washout status, temperature set point, and continuous-run expectations.
    • Pre-cover produce-adjacent lanes.
    • Avoid trying to buy reefer like van.
  • Best interpretation: reefer has less negotiating room than the other modes today.

📦 LTL/Partial

  • Market read: 8,244 loads at $1.65/mile paid
  • What matters: this is a relationship-defense product in a high-fuel market.
  • Broker edge today:
    • Offer it to customers as a structured alternative, not a cheap surprise.
    • Be explicit about touches, consolidation timing, and wider transit windows.
  • Best use cases: cost-sensitive retail, light manufacturing support, and freight that can tolerate schedule flexibility.

💬 How to sell this market to customers today

  • Lead with execution, not fear.

    • Best message: “This is still a coverable market, but the truck has to be priced for fuel, route variance, and appointment risk.”

  • Use transparent quote structure.

    • Linehaul
    • Fuel
    • Detention
    • Weather or detour exposure

  • Why it works: customers fight a big all-in number more than they fight a clearly justified cost stack.

  • Give customers choices instead of one disputed rate.

    • Premium truckload: highest service confidence
    • Flex truckload: wider pickup/delivery window at a lower buy
    • LTL (Less Than Truckload) / Partial: lower cost with wider transit tolerance

  • Why it works: in volatile conditions, customers want control as much as price.

  • Call the shippers most likely to get dropped over the weekend.

    • Midwest industrial
    • Automotive support freight
    • Chicago-area replenishment
    • Produce-adjacent reefer accounts
  • The psychology here matters: shippers who feel the routing guide may fail are far more open to premium pricing before service breaks than after.

🤝 How to buy trucks today without giving away margin

  • Buy positioned capacity, not theoretical capacity.

    • The farther the deadhead, the more the carrier prices fuel, weather, and reload uncertainty.

  • Ask sharper questions before awarding the load.

    • Where is the truck right now?
    • What was the last delivery?
    • What route will the driver actually run?
    • Do Hours of Service (HOS) support the commitment?
    • What is the next reload market?

  • Use accelerated settlement as a real sourcing tool.

    • In a high-fuel environment, smaller carriers often value cash-turn speed more than a tiny linehaul increase.
    • That is especially effective on regional Midwest freight and short-haul lanes.

  • Sell the carrier the roundtrip logic.

    • Inbound Southeast
    • Ohio Valley loops
    • Chicago replenishment turns
    • Project freight with known reload geography

  • Good carriers are buying the whole trip, not just the first leg.

  • Do not overreact to posted rates.

    • Since paid is below posted in every mode today, a broker who simply “matches the board” is likely overbuying ordinary freight.
    • The right move is lane-by-lane discipline, not blanket aggression.

🛡️ Risk controls that matter today

  • Weather risk: verify shipper and receiver access directly.

    • Interstate open does not mean yard open, dock open, or truck route open.

  • Accessorial risk: put special attention on:

    • Detention
    • Layover
    • Weather detour
    • Appointment failure caused by flood routing

  • Reason: most weekend margin leakage in this market will come from poor terms, not just bad linehaul.

  • Fraud and carrier-swap risk: tighten controls.

    • Treat these as stop-and-verify events:
    • New dispatcher number
    • Changed driver phone
    • Changed bank details
    • Changed email domain
    • Last-minute truck substitution
    • Verify through a known-good contact path, not the new contact information you just received.

  • Compliance risk: FMCSA (Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration) Clearinghouse enforcement is still shrinking the legal driver pool.

    • Reconfirm the assigned driver and operating authority close to pickup.
    • On critical freight, do not solve service risk by lowering your compliance standards.
