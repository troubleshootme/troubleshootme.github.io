📊 Decoding the Post-Holiday Volume Surge and Rate Spread

Real-time transactional data reveals a significant post-holiday rebound, with total available loads surging 10.5% overnight to 179,811. This volume influx is driving notable divergence in rate spreads across equipment types. In the temperature-controlled sector, paid rates of $3.29/mile exceed posted rates of $3.09/mile by $0.20, signaling that carriers hold substantial leverage as peak summer produce season ramps up. Conversely, dry van paid rates of $2.51/mile sit below the posted average of $2.64/mile, offering brokers a $0.13/mile margin window. Flatbed equipment also shows a favorable $0.07/mile broker advantage, with paid rates averaging $3.52/mile against a $3.59/mile posted average. Brokers must leverage these spreads to secure capacity while protecting margins, particularly on lanes where carrier premiums are expanding.

📈 Rate Velocity Analysis: Dry Van and Flatbed Divergence

Spot market rate velocity is exhibiting highly unusual patterns as the industry transitions into June. Dry van spot rates have surged over the past month, coming within three cents of their all-time historic high set in late 2021. This rapid upward momentum is driven by a post-holiday capacity pullback, with equipment postings dropping significantly. Meanwhile, flatbed linehaul rates have set consecutive weekly records, with the national average spot linehaul rate reaching historic highs. High diesel prices, currently averaging $5.448/gallon, continue to act as a structural floor for these rates, as carriers refuse to negotiate below their operating cost thresholds. Brokers must expect rates to remain sticky at these elevated levels, requiring immediate adjustments to shipper contract pricing to prevent routing guide failures.

🔧 Driver Shortages and Regulatory Pressures Restructuring Capacity

Carrier-side capacity is undergoing a structural transformation driven by a combination of persistent driver shortages, regulatory enforcement, and high operating costs. A recent nationwide survey of school and commercial transportation leaders highlights that driver shortages remain the primary barrier to operational efficiency, forcing fleets to run under capacity. This shortage is compounded by strict regulatory compliance, as evidenced by recent FMCSA rules restricting non-domiciled CDLs, which has prompted legal challenges and exemption requests from affected drivers. Furthermore, high fuel costs are accelerating the exit of smaller owner-operators, leading to the rise of underutilized or 'ghost' fleets. For brokers, these dynamics mean that reliable, compliant capacity is becoming increasingly scarce, necessitating rigorous carrier vetting and stronger relationships with established asset-based partners.

🚚 Mode-by-mode broker playbook

🚛 Dry Van

  • Market read

    • 25,416 available loads
    • $2.64/mile posted
    • $2.51/mile paid
    • 3,708 loads moved today
    • Van is the cleanest standard truckload buying opportunity, but only by $0.13/mile. That is a narrow cushion, not a loose market.

  • What to do today

    • Cover fixed-appointment freight early.
    • The later the day goes, the more likely tomorrow’s quote is based on replacement cost, not this morning’s screen.
    • Use short quote validity.
    • In this market, a quote should expire in hours, not a full business day.
    • Buy local executable trucks, not distant cheap trucks.
    • Diesel is punishing deadhead, so a carrier 120 miles away is not equivalent to a truck already near the shipper.

  • Where brokers usually get hurt

    • Treating a $0.13 spread like a cushion.
    • It is not a cushion if the carrier has poor reload prospects, poor appointment fit, or weather exposure.
    • Selling tomorrow off today’s board.
    • That is how routing guide failures turn into margin givebacks.

❄️ Reefer

  • Market read

    • $3.09/mile posted
    • $3.29/mile paid
    • 1,114 loads moved today

  • What to do today

    • Re-price Southeast, California, and produce-adjacent freight now.
    • Blueberries, peaches, tomatoes, watermelon, and corn are all pulling equipment into time-sensitive agricultural networks.

🧠 Shipper and carrier psychology: how to win the conversation

  • What shippers are thinking

    • They see a board and believe today’s market is broadly available.
    • They may focus on posted numbers and assume that is executable.

  • How to frame it

    • “Today’s opportunity exists, but it is lane-specific and time-sensitive. If we miss today’s coverage window, tomorrow’s replacement cost is likely higher.”
    • That message is honest, credible, and commercially useful.

  • What carriers are thinking

    • They are not just asking, “What’s the rate?”
    • They are asking:
    • How far is the deadhead?
    • Will I get loaded on time?
    • How bad is the receiver?
    • What does this do for my reload?
    • How much fuel risk am I eating?

  • How to get better carrier response

    • Lead with precision.
    • exact pickup time
    • exact delivery time
    • commodity
    • weight
    • access conditions
    • detention policy
    • reload logic
    • In a tight market, precision converts faster than optimism.

⚠️ Risk controls that protect margin this week

  • For flood-exposed freight

    • Put detention, layover, reroute, redelivery, and TONU (Truck Ordered Not Used) terms in writing before dispatch.
    • Build appointment buffers instead of apologizing later.

  • For reefer

    • Confirm washout, pre-cool, setpoint, seal process, and commodity details before final pricing.
    • Do not assume a produce-adjacent load will cover like a normal food load.

  • For flatbed and heavy haul

    • Confirm site conditions and path feasibility before rate finalization.
    • Ask about loading equipment, securement expectations, and daylight restrictions.

  • For all new or questionable carriers

    • Tighten compliance checks.
    • With regulatory pressure and “ghost fleet” concerns in the market, this is the wrong environment to get lazy on verification.

  • For LTL/Partial

    • Quote the accessorial profile, not just the base rate.
    • Margin leaks here are usually administrative, not linehaul.
